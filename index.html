Webpage for the Linguistic Atlas Project

This Linguistic Atlas Project (LAP) webpage features responses to a question about case furniture. Featuring an interactive map created with QGIS and Carto, this page offers users the chance to explore LAP data and find out more about the responses featured on the map.

LANE Map of 'Bureau' Responses

The Linguistic Atlas of New England was published (LANE) as a series of lithograph maps in the mid-1930s. Featured here is the LANE map of responses to a question about the piece of furniture with drawers often found in bedrooms. The original LANE maps are rare and difficult to find. Using QGIS to georeference and add points to a high-resolution scan of a vintage LANE map, this concept represents a marriage between the old and the new.

Appalachian Trail

Ok, so this one's not about language, but it does feature the methods and color palette that I favor. A Raisz Landform Map, created by Erwin Raisz (1893-1968) was georeferenced and supplemented with geographic points from the Appalachian Trail Conservancy to highlight the path of the Trail through North Carolina, Tennessee, and Virginia.

About Me

Hi, I'm Allison. I'm a linguist who specializes in dialect geography and variation in language. I'm new to digital mapping, but as the Editor of the Linguistic Atlas Project (LAP), I wanted to learn how to better utilize the data that I have as well the digital libraries and mapping resources available today. A lot of LAP data is 'old' but that doesn't mean our interactions with and understanding of that data need to be!

I'm also a mom to kids and dogs, a teacher, and a decent cook. Right now I'm working on a book for 'normal people' about words for things around the house.
